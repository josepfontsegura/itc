Exercises: 1. Sequences of random variables

Exercise 1.1.

A memoryless source over the alphabet $\{0,1,2\}$ has $P_U(2)=\frac{1}{2}$. Knowing that the probability that the source outputs the sequence $210$ is $\frac{1}{36}$, what is the probability that the source outputs the sequence $02$?

(a) $\frac{1}{12}$
(b) $\frac{1}{6}$
(c) Both (a) and (b) are compatible answers.

Show solution

The correct answer is (c) ★

The statement gives $P_U(2)=\frac{1}{2}$ and therefore $P_{U_1U_2U_3}(210)=\frac{1}{2}P_U(1)P_U(0)=\frac{1}{36}$. Together with $P_U(0)+P_U(1)=\frac{1}{2}$, we find that the distribution is either $\bigl\{\frac{1}{6},\frac{1}{3},\frac{1}{2}\bigr\}$ or $\bigl\{\frac{1}{3},\frac{1}{6},\frac{1}{2}\bigr\}$. The question asks for $P_U(0)P_U(2)$, which can be either $\frac{1}{12}$ or $\frac{1}{6}$.

Exercise 1.2.

What is the probability that the source does not output the all-$2$ sequence?

(a) $\frac{1}{2^n}$
(b) $\frac{1}{4^n}$
(c) $1-2^{-n}$

Show solution

The correct answer is (c) ★

Since the probability that the source outputs the all-$2$ sequence is $P_{U_1\cdots U_n}(2\cdots 2)=P_U(2)^n=2^{-n}$, the complementary event happens with probability $1-2^{-n}$.

Exercise 1.3.

Which of the following pairs of sequences do not have the same probability?

(a) $0012$ and $0201$
(b) $1111$ and $2222$
(c) $1212$ and $2121$

Show solution

The correct answer is (b) ★

As a discrete memoryless source, the probability of a sequence only depends on the number of occurrences of each symbol.

Exercise 1.4.

A discrete memoryless channel has input alphabet $\{a,b,c\}$ and output alphabet $\{a,b\}$, with transition probabilities $P_{Y|X}(a|a)=\frac{1}{3}$, $P_{Y|X}(b|a)=\frac{2}{3}$, $P_{Y|X}(a|b)=0$, $P_{Y|X}(b|b)=1$, $P_{Y|X}(a|c)=\frac{1}{2}$ and $P_{Y|X}(b|c)=\frac{1}{2}$. What is the probability that the output sequence is $abb$ conditioned on the input sequence being $cba$?

(a) $0$
(b) $\frac{1}{3}$
(c) None of the others.

Show solution

The correct answer is (b) ★

The probability is given by $$ P_{Y|X}(a|c)P_{Y|X}(b|b)P_{Y|X}(b|a) = \frac{1}{2} \cdot 1 \cdot \frac{2}{3} = \frac{1}{3}. $$

Exercise 1.5.

For the same channel as in Exercise 1.4, knowing that $(X_1,X_2,X_3)=bbb$, what is the probability that $(Y_1,Y_2,Y_3)$ is $aaa$?

(a) $1$
(b) $0$
(c) $\frac{1}{8}$

Show solution

The correct answer is (b) ★

Conditioned on $(X_1,X_2,X_3)=bbb$, the probability that the output sequence is $aaa$ is $\mathbb{P}[(Y_1,Y_2,Y_3)=aaa\mid(X_1,X_2,X_3)=bbb] = P_{Y_1Y_2Y_3|X_1X_2X_3}(aaa|bbb)$. Since the channel is memoryless, the conditional distribution is given by $P_{Y_1Y_2Y_3|X_1X_2X_3}(aaa|bbb)=P_{Y|X}(a|b)^3=0^3=0$.

Exercise 1.6.

For the same channel as in Exercise 1.4, suppose the input $(X_1,X_2,X_3)$ is uniformly distributed over $\{aaa,bbb,ccc\}$ (each with probability $\frac{1}{3}$). What is the probability that $(Y_1,Y_2,Y_3)=bbb$?

(a) $\frac{59}{648}$
(b) $\frac{307}{648}$
(c) $\frac{35}{648}$

Show solution

The correct answer is (b) ★

By the total probability law, we can compute the probability that $(Y_1,Y_2,Y_3)$ is $bbb$ as $\sum_{(x_1,x_2,x_3)\in\{a,b,c\}^3} \mathbb{P}[(Y_1,Y_2,Y_3)=bbb\mid(X_1,X_2,X_3)=(x_1,x_2,x_3)]P_{X_1X_2X_3}(x_1,x_2,x_3)$. We know $(X_1,X_2,X_3)$ only takes three possible values with non-zero probability, so we only have to compute the following probabilities: $$ \mathbb{P}[(Y_1,Y_2,Y_3)=bbb\mid(X_1,X_2,X_3)=aaa] = \tfrac{8}{27},\\ \mathbb{P}[(Y_1,Y_2,Y_3)=bbb\mid(X_1,X_2,X_3)=bbb] = 1,\\ \mathbb{P}[(Y_1,Y_2,Y_3)=bbb\mid(X_1,X_2,X_3)=ccc] = \tfrac{1}{8}. $$ For example, to find the first one we used that $P_{Y_1Y_2Y_3|X_1X_2X_3}(bbb|aaa)=P_{Y|X}(b|a)^3$. Plugging the numbers into the summation and noting that $P_{X_1X_2X_3}(bbb)=P_{X_1X_2X_3}(ccc)=P_{X_1X_2X_3}(aaa)=\frac{1}{3}$, we obtain the result.

Exercise 1.7.

The distribution of $(U_1,U_2)$, in lexicographic order, is given by

(a) $\bigl\{ \frac{1}{4},\frac{1}{12},\frac{1}{6},\frac{1}{12},\frac{1}{36},\frac{1}{18},\frac{1}{6},\frac{1}{18},\frac{1}{9} \bigr\}$
(b) $\bigl\{ \frac{1}{9}, \frac{1}{18}, \frac{1}{6}, \frac{1}{18},\frac{1}{36} , \frac{1}{12}, \frac{1}{6}, \frac{1}{12}, \frac{1}{4}\bigr\}$
(c) None of the others.

Show solution

The correct answer is (a) ★

Together with $P_U(0)+P_U(1)+P_U(2)=1$, we find that $P_U(0)=\frac{1}{2}$, $P_U(1)=\frac{1}{6}$ and $P_U(2)=\frac{1}{3}$. Since symbols are i.i.d., the distribution of $(U_1,U_2)$ satisfies $P_{U_1U_2}(u_1,u_2)=P_U(u_1)P_U(u_2)$, for each $(u_1,u_2)\in\{0,1,2\}^2=\{00,01,02,10,11,12,20,21,22\}$, leading to the result.

Exercise 1.8.

A memoryless source over the alphabet $\{0,1,2\}$ has distribution $\{P_U(0),P_U(1),P_U(2)\}=\bigl\{\frac{1}{2},\frac{1}{3},\frac{1}{6}\bigr\}$ and emits two symbols $(U_1,U_2)$. What is the probability that the source outputs a sequence containing at least one symbol $0$?

(a) $\frac{3}{4}$
(b) $\frac{1}{2}$
(c) $\frac{4}{9}$

Show solution

The correct answer is (a) ★

Use the complementary event: $\mathbb{P}[\text{at least one }0]=1-\mathbb{P}[\text{no }0]=1-(1-P_U(0))^2=1-\bigl(1-\tfrac{1}{2}\bigr)^2=1-\tfrac14=\tfrac34$. Equivalently, the event is the set ${\cal A}=\{00,01,02,10,20\}$ and $\sum_{(u_1,u_2)\in{\cal A}}P_{U_1U_2}(u_1,u_2)=\tfrac34$.

Exercise 1.9.

Which is the probability that the source outputs the all-$0$ sequence?

(a) $\frac{1}{3^n}$
(b) $\frac{1}{2^n}$
(c) $\frac{1}{6^n}$

Show solution

The correct answer is (b) ★

As a sequence of i.i.d. random variables, we have that $\mathbb{P}[(U_1,\ldots,U_n)=0\cdots 0]=P_{U_1\cdots U_n}(0\cdots 0) = \prod_{i=1}^n P_U(0) = \frac{1}{2^n}.$

Exercise 1.10.

Let $X$ be the channel input with distribution $P_X$ over $\{a,b,c\}$, and let $Y$ over $\{a,b\}$ be the output of the channel $P_{Y|X}$ given by $P_{Y|X}(a|a)=\frac{1}{3}$, $P_{Y|X}(b|a)=\frac{2}{3}$, $P_{Y|X}(a|b)=0$, $P_{Y|X}(b|b)=1$, $P_{Y|X}(a|c)=\frac{1}{2}$, $P_{Y|X}(b|c)=\frac{1}{2}$. Which of the following corresponds to the probability distribution of $Y$?

(a) $\bigl\{ \frac 13P_X(a)+P_X(b)+\frac 12 P_X(c) , \frac 23 P_X(a) + \frac 12 P_X(c) \bigr\}$
(b) $\bigl\{ \frac 13P_X(a)+\frac 12 P_X(c) , \frac 23 P_X(a) + P_X(b) + \frac 12 P_X(c) \bigr\}$
(c) None of the others

Show solution

The correct answer is (b) ★

The probability distribution of $Y$ is given by $\{P_Y(a),P_Y(b)\}$. By the total probability law, $P_Y(y) = \sum_{x\in\{a,b,c\}}P_{Y|X}(y|x)P_X(x)$. For example, $P_Y(a) = P_{Y|X}(a|a)P_X(a) + P_{Y|X}(a|b)P_X(b) + P_{Y|X}(a|c)P_X(c)=\frac13P_X(a)+\frac12P_X(c)$. Substituting the numbers we obtain the result.

Exercise 1.11.

Consider the memoryless channel $P_{Y|X}$ over $\{a,b,c\}\to\{a,b\}$ with $P_{Y|X}(a|a)=\frac{1}{3}$, $P_{Y|X}(a|b)=0$ and $P_{Y|X}(a|c)=\frac{1}{2}$, used $3$ times. The input $(X_1,X_2,X_3)$ is uniform over $\{aaa,bbb,ccc\}$, that is, each of these three sequences has probability $\frac{1}{3}$. Which is the probability that $(Y_1,Y_2,Y_3)=aaa$?

(a) $\frac{59}{648}$
(b) $\frac{59}{324}$
(c) $\frac{35}{648}$

Show solution

The correct answer is (c) ★

By the total probability law, we can compute the probability that $(Y_1,Y_2,Y_3)$ is $aaa$ as $\sum_{(x_1,x_2,x_3)\in\{a,b,c\}^3} \mathbb{P}[(Y_1,Y_2,Y_3)=aaa\mid(X_1,X_2,X_3)=(x_1,x_2,x_3)]\,P_{X_1X_2X_3}(x_1,x_2,x_3)$. We know $(X_1,X_2,X_3)$ only takes three possible values with non-zero probability, so we only have to compute the following probabilities: $$\mathbb{P}[(Y_1,Y_2,Y_3)=aaa\mid(X_1,X_2,X_3)=aaa] = \tfrac{1}{27},\quad \mathbb{P}[(Y_1,Y_2,Y_3)=aaa\mid(X_1,X_2,X_3)=bbb] = 0,\quad \mathbb{P}[(Y_1,Y_2,Y_3)=aaa\mid(X_1,X_2,X_3)=ccc] = \tfrac 18.$$ For example, to find the first one we used that $P_{Y_1Y_2Y_3|X_1X_2X_3}(aaa\mid aaa)=P_{Y|X}(a\mid a)^3$. Plugging the numbers into the summation and noting that $P_{X_1X_2X_3}(bbb)=P_{X_1X_2X_3}(ccc)=P_{X_1X_2X_3}(aaa)=\frac 13$, we obtain $\frac{1}{3}\bigl(\frac{1}{27}+0+\frac18\bigr)=\frac{35}{648}$.

Exercise 1.12.

Let $X$ and $Y$ be two independent random variables with $X\in\{0,1\}$ equiprobable and $Y\in\{0,1,2\}$ equiprobable. What is $\mathbb{E}[XY]$?

(a) $\tfrac{1}{2}$
(b) $1$
(c) $\tfrac{1}{3}$

Show solution

The correct answer is (a) ★

By independence, $\mathbb{E}[XY]=\mathbb{E}[X]\,\mathbb{E}[Y]=\tfrac{1}{2}\cdot 1 = \tfrac{1}{2}$.

Exercise 1.13.

A ternary source emits symbols from $\{0,1,2\}$ with probabilities $\{\tfrac{1}{2},\tfrac{1}{4},\tfrac{1}{4}\}$. For a sequence of length $n=2$, how many distinct sequences have probability $\tfrac{1}{8}$?

(a) $2$
(b) $4$
(c) $1$

Show solution

The correct answer is (b) ★

A sequence has probability $\tfrac{1}{8}$ when it consists of one symbol with probability $\tfrac{1}{2}$ and one with probability $\tfrac{1}{4}$. The possible sequences are $01$, $02$, $10$, $20$, giving $4$ sequences.

Exercise 1.14.

A discrete memoryless source emits i.i.d. symbols from $\{a,b,c\}$ with $P(a)=\tfrac{1}{2}$, $P(b)=\tfrac{1}{3}$, $P(c)=\tfrac{1}{6}$. What is the probability that a sequence of length $2$ contains no $a$?

(a) $\tfrac{1}{4}$
(b) $\tfrac{1}{2}$
(c) $\tfrac{2}{9}$

Show solution

The correct answer is (a) ★

The probability of no $a$ in a single symbol is $1-\tfrac{1}{2}=\tfrac{1}{2}$. For two independent symbols, the probability is $\bigl(\tfrac{1}{2}\bigr)^2=\tfrac{1}{4}$.

Exercise 1.15.

Consider a binary symmetric channel with crossover probability $p=\tfrac{1}{4}$. If the input is $X=0$, what is the probability that the output sequence $(Y_1,Y_2)$ equals $(0,1)$ when two independent uses of the channel are made?

(a) $\tfrac{3}{16}$
(b) $\tfrac{1}{4}$
(c) $\tfrac{1}{16}$

Show solution

The correct answer is (a) ★

Since the channel is memoryless, $P(Y_1=0,Y_2=1\mid X_1=0,X_2=0)=P(Y=0\mid X=0)\,P(Y=1\mid X=0)=(1-p)\,p=\tfrac{3}{4}\cdot\tfrac{1}{4}=\tfrac{3}{16}$.

Exercise 1.17.

Let $(X_1,X_2,X_3)$ be i.i.d. with $P(X_i=0)=\tfrac{1}{3}$ and $P(X_i=1)=\tfrac{2}{3}$. What is the most probable sequence?

Exercise 1.18.

A discrete memoryless channel has input $X\in\{0,1\}$ and output $Y=X$. If $P_X(0)=\tfrac{3}{4}$, what is $P_Y(1)$?

(a) $\tfrac{1}{4}$
(b) $\tfrac{3}{4}$
(c) $\tfrac{1}{2}$

Since $Y=X$ deterministically, $P_Y(1)=P_X(1)=1-\tfrac{3}{4}=\tfrac{1}{4}$.

Exercise 1.19.

Two random variables $X\in\{0,1\}$ and $Y\in\{0,1\}$ satisfy $P_{XY}(0,0)=P_{XY}(1,1)=\tfrac{1}{2}$. Are $X$ and $Y$ independent?

(a) No, because $P_{XY}(0,0)\neq P_X(0)P_Y(0)$
(b) Yes, because $P_X(0)=P_Y(0)=\tfrac{1}{2}$
(c) It depends on the marginals

Show solution

The correct answer is (a) ★

We have $P_X(0)=P_Y(0)=\tfrac{1}{2}$, so $P_X(0)P_Y(0)=\tfrac{1}{4}\neq\tfrac{1}{2}=P_{XY}(0,0)$. Therefore $X$ and $Y$ are not independent.

Exercise 1.20.

Let $X$ be a random variable taking values in $\{1,2,3,4\}$ with equal probability. What is $\operatorname{var}(X)$?

(a) $\tfrac{5}{4}$
(b) $\tfrac{5}{3}$
(c) $\tfrac{3}{2}$

Show solution

The correct answer is (a) ★

We have $\mathbb{E}[X]=\tfrac{10}{4}=\tfrac{5}{2}$ and $\mathbb{E}[X^2]=\tfrac{1+4+9+16}{4}=\tfrac{30}{4}=\tfrac{15}{2}$. Therefore $\operatorname{var}(X)=\tfrac{15}{2}-\tfrac{25}{4}=\tfrac{5}{4}$.

Exercise 1.21.

A discrete memoryless source emits i.i.d. symbols over $\mathcal{X}=\{a,b,c,d\}$, with $P_X(a)=3\,P_X(b)$ and $P_X(b)=P_X(c)=P_X(d)$.

What is the full distribution $P_X$?

(a) $P_X(a)=\tfrac12,\ P_X(b)=P_X(c)=P_X(d)=\tfrac16$
(b) $P_X(a)=\tfrac13,\ P_X(b)=P_X(c)=P_X(d)=\tfrac29$
(c) $P_X(a)=\tfrac34,\ P_X(b)=P_X(c)=P_X(d)=\tfrac{1}{12}$
(d) $P_X(a)=\tfrac14,\ P_X(b)=P_X(c)=P_X(d)=\tfrac14$

Show solution

The correct answer is (a) ★

Let $p=P_X(b)=P_X(c)=P_X(d)$, so $P_X(a)=3p$. Normalization requires $3p+p+p+p=6p=1$, hence $p=\tfrac16$ and $P_X(a)=3p=\tfrac12$. Check: $\tfrac12+3\cdot\tfrac16=1$.

Exercise 1.22.

A DMS over $\mathcal{X}=\{a,b,c,d\}$ has $P_X(a)=\tfrac12$ and $P_X(b)=P_X(c)=P_X(d)=\tfrac16$.

Compute the probability of a fixed sequence of length $n=5$ that contains $a$ twice and each of $b,c,d$ once.

(a) $\dfrac{1}{864}\approx 1.16\times10^{-3}$
(b) $\dfrac{5}{72}\approx 6.94\times10^{-2}$
(c) $\dfrac{1}{216}\approx 4.63\times10^{-3}$
(d) $\dfrac{1}{432}\approx 2.31\times10^{-3}$

Show solution

The correct answer is (a) ★

For a fixed sequence the i.i.d. product rule applies with no multinomial factor: $\mathbb{P}[\boldsymbol{x}]=P_X(a)^2P_X(b)P_X(c)P_X(d)=(\tfrac12)^2(\tfrac16)^3=\tfrac14\cdot\tfrac{1}{216}=\tfrac{1}{864}$. Multiplying by the count $\binom{5}{2,1,1,1}=60$ would give $\tfrac{5}{72}$, but that is the probability of the whole type, not of one fixed sequence.

Exercise 1.23.

A DMS over $\mathcal{X}=\{a,b,c,d\}$ has $P_X(a)=\tfrac12$ and $P_X(b)=P_X(c)=P_X(d)=\tfrac16$.

For a fixed length $n$, which sequence is the most probable, and what is its probability?

(a) The all-$a$ string $(a,\dots,a)$, with probability $(1/2)^n$
(b) A sequence with $a,b,c,d$ in proportion to their probabilities, with probability $(1/2)^{n/2}(1/6)^{n/2}$
(c) Any sequence is equally probable, with probability $(1/4)^n$
(d) The all-$b$ string $(b,\dots,b)$, with probability $(1/6)^n$

Show solution

The correct answer is (a) ★

The probability factorizes as $\prod_{i=1}^{n}P_X(x_i)$, so it is maximized term by term by picking, in every position, the most likely symbol $a$ ($P_X(a)=\tfrac12$). Replacing any $x_i\neq a$ by $a$ raises that factor from $\tfrac16$ to $\tfrac12$, strictly increasing the product. The unique maximizer is the all-$a$ word with probability $(1/2)^n$.

Exercise 1.24.

A discrete memoryless source emits i.i.d. symbols over $\mathcal{X}=\{a,b\}$ with $\mathbb{P}(a)=\frac{1}{3}$ and $\mathbb{P}(b)=\frac{2}{3}$.

What is the probability of the sequence $abba$?

(a) $\frac{4}{81}$
(b) $\frac{2}{81}$
(c) $\frac{8}{81}$
(d) $\frac{1}{16}$

Show solution

The correct answer is (a) ★

For a memoryless source the sequence probability factorises: $\mathbb{P}(abba)=\mathbb{P}(a)\mathbb{P}(b)\mathbb{P}(b)\mathbb{P}(a)=\frac13\cdot\frac23\cdot\frac23\cdot\frac13$. This equals $\left(\frac13\right)^2\left(\frac23\right)^2=\frac{1\cdot 4}{81}=\frac{4}{81}\approx0.0494$.

Exercise 1.25.

A discrete memoryless source emits i.i.d. symbols over $\mathcal{X}=\{a,b\}$ with $\mathbb{P}(a)=\frac{1}{3}$ and $\mathbb{P}(b)=\frac{2}{3}$.

What is the probability that a length-$4$ sequence contains exactly two $a$'s?

(a) $\frac{8}{27}$
(b) $\frac{4}{81}$
(c) $\frac{16}{81}$
(d) $\frac{4}{27}$

Show solution

The correct answer is (a) ★

Each specific sequence with two $a$'s and two $b$'s has probability $\mathbb{P}(a)^2\mathbb{P}(b)^2=\frac{4}{81}$. The number of such arrangements is $\binom{4}{2}=6$. Summing the equiprobable, mutually exclusive sequences gives $6\cdot\frac{4}{81}=\frac{24}{81}=\frac{8}{27}\approx0.296$.

Exercise 1.26.

A discrete memoryless source emits i.i.d. symbols over $\mathcal{X}=\{a,b\}$ with $\mathbb{P}(a)=\frac{1}{3}$ and $\mathbb{P}(b)=\frac{2}{3}$.

What is the most probable length-$4$ sequence, and what is its probability?

(a) $bbbb$, with probability $\frac{16}{81}$
(b) $aaaa$, with probability $\frac{1}{81}$
(c) $abba$, with probability $\frac{4}{81}$
(d) $bbbb$, with probability $\frac{8}{27}$

Show solution

The correct answer is (a) ★

A sequence's probability depends only on its symbol counts. Since $\mathbb{P}(b)=\frac23>\frac13=\mathbb{P}(a)$, replacing an $a$ by a $b$ multiplies the probability by $2>1$, so the single most probable sequence uses only $b$'s: $bbbb$, with $\left(\frac23\right)^4=\frac{16}{81}\approx0.198$. (Note $\frac{8}{27}$ is the probability of the two-$a$ event, which bundles 6 sequences, not a single one.)

Exercise 1.27.

Let $X$ and $Y$ take values in $\{0,1\}$ with joint distribution $P_{XY}(0,0)=P_{XY}(1,1)=\tfrac{1}{3}$ and $P_{XY}(0,1)=P_{XY}(1,0)=\tfrac{1}{6}$.

What are the marginal distributions $P_X$ and $P_Y$?

(a) $P_X=P_Y=\big(\tfrac{1}{2},\tfrac{1}{2}\big)$
(b) $P_X=P_Y=\big(\tfrac{1}{3},\tfrac{2}{3}\big)$
(c) $P_X=P_Y=\big(\tfrac{2}{3},\tfrac{1}{3}\big)$
(d) $P_X=\big(\tfrac{1}{3},\tfrac{1}{6}\big),\ P_Y=\big(\tfrac{1}{6},\tfrac{1}{3}\big)$

Show solution

The correct answer is (a) ★

Marginalize by summing each row (for $P_X$) and column (for $P_Y$): $P_X(0)=\tfrac{1}{3}+\tfrac{1}{6}=\tfrac{1}{2}$ and $P_X(1)=\tfrac{1}{6}+\tfrac{1}{3}=\tfrac{1}{2}$. By the symmetry of the table the columns give the same, so $P_X=P_Y=\big(\tfrac{1}{2},\tfrac{1}{2}\big)$ (both uniform on $\{0,1\}$).

Exercise 1.28.

Let $X,Y\in\{0,1\}$ have joint distribution $P_{XY}(0,0)=P_{XY}(1,1)=\tfrac{1}{3}$, $P_{XY}(0,1)=P_{XY}(1,0)=\tfrac{1}{6}$, with marginals $P_X=P_Y=\big(\tfrac{1}{2},\tfrac{1}{2}\big)$.

Are $X$ and $Y$ independent?

(a) No, because $P_{XY}(0,0)=\tfrac{1}{3}\neq\tfrac{1}{4}=P_X(0)P_Y(0)$
(b) Yes, because the marginals are both uniform
(c) Yes, because the table is symmetric
(d) No, because $P_X(0)\neq P_Y(0)$

Show solution

The correct answer is (a) ★

Independence requires $P_{XY}(x,y)=P_X(x)P_Y(y)$ for every pair. Testing $(0,0)$: $P_X(0)P_Y(0)=\tfrac{1}{2}\cdot\tfrac{1}{2}=\tfrac{1}{4}$, but $P_{XY}(0,0)=\tfrac{1}{3}$. Since $\tfrac{1}{4}\neq\tfrac{1}{3}$ the factorization fails, so $X$ and $Y$ are not independent. (Uniform marginals or a symmetric table do not by themselves imply independence.)

Exercise 1.29.

Let $X,Y\in\{0,1\}$ have joint distribution $P_{XY}(0,0)=P_{XY}(1,1)=\tfrac{1}{3}$, $P_{XY}(0,1)=P_{XY}(1,0)=\tfrac{1}{6}$, with $P_X(0)=\tfrac{1}{2}$.

Compute $\mathbb{P}(Y=1\mid X=0)$.

(a) $\tfrac{1}{3}$
(b) $\tfrac{1}{6}$
(c) $\tfrac{2}{3}$
(d) $\tfrac{1}{2}$

Show solution

The correct answer is (a) ★

By the definition of conditional probability, $\mathbb{P}(Y=1\mid X=0)=\dfrac{P_{XY}(0,1)}{P_X(0)}=\dfrac{1/6}{1/2}=\tfrac{2}{6}=\tfrac{1}{3}$. (The joint value $\tfrac{1}{6}$ is the un-normalized weight; dividing by $P_X(0)=\tfrac{1}{2}$ gives $\tfrac{1}{3}$. Note this differs from $P_Y(1)=\tfrac{1}{2}$, confirming non-independence.)

Exercise 1.30.

A binary asymmetric channel has crossover probabilities $\delta_0 = 0.8$ (probability that input $0$ flips to $1$) and $\delta_1 = 0.06$ (probability that input $1$ flips to $0$), with uniform input $P_X(0)=P_X(1)=\tfrac12$.

Using Bayes' rule, what is the a posteriori probability $P_{X\mid Y}(0\mid 1)$ that a $0$ was sent, given that a $1$ was received?

(a) $\dfrac{40}{87}\approx 0.460$
(b) $\dfrac{47}{87}\approx 0.540$
(c) $0.8$
(d) $0.4$

Show solution

The correct answer is (a) ★

By Bayes' rule, $P_{X\mid Y}(0\mid 1)=\dfrac{P_{Y\mid X}(1\mid 0)\,P_X(0)}{P_Y(1)}$. The marginal from the law of total probability is $P_Y(1)=0.8\cdot\tfrac12+0.94\cdot\tfrac12=0.87$. Thus $P_{X\mid Y}(0\mid 1)=\dfrac{0.8\cdot 0.5}{0.87}=\dfrac{0.4}{0.87}=\dfrac{40}{87}\approx 0.460$. (The value $\tfrac{47}{87}$ is the complementary posterior $P_{X\mid Y}(1\mid 1)$; $0.4$ forgets to divide by $P_Y(1)$.)

Exercise 1.31.

A binary asymmetric channel has crossover probabilities $\delta_0 = 0.8$ (probability that input $0$ flips to $1$) and $\delta_1 = 0.06$ (probability that input $1$ flips to $0$), with uniform input $P_X(0)=P_X(1)=\tfrac12$.

What is the joint probability $P_{XY}(1,0)$ that a $1$ was sent and a $0$ was received?

(a) $0.03$
(b) $0.06$
(c) $0.40$
(d) $0.47$

Show solution

The correct answer is (a) ★

By the chain rule, the joint factorises as prior times channel transition: $P_{XY}(1,0)=P_{Y\mid X}(0\mid 1)\,P_X(1)$. Here $P_{Y\mid X}(0\mid 1)=\delta_1=0.06$ (the $1\to 0$ crossover) and $P_X(1)=\tfrac12$, giving $P_{XY}(1,0)=0.06\cdot 0.5=0.03$. (The value $0.06$ is the conditional alone, not multiplied by the prior.)

Exercise 1.32.

A sequence $(X_1,\ldots,X_n)$ (with $n\ge 2$) takes only the values $00\cdots0$ and $11\cdots1$, each with probability $\frac12$. Is $(X_1,\ldots,X_n)$ a sequence of i.i.d. random variables?

(a) False: the bits are identically $\mathrm{Bernoulli}(\tfrac12)$ but not independent
(b) True: each $X_i$ is $\mathrm{Bernoulli}(\tfrac12)$, so the sequence is i.i.d.
(c) True: the joint probability factors as $\frac{1}{2^n}$
(d) False: the bits are not even identically distributed

Show solution

The correct answer is (a) ★

Each marginal is $P_{X_i}(0)=P_{X_i}(1)=\frac12$, so the bits are identically distributed. For i.i.d. we also need independence, i.e. $P_{X_1\cdots X_n}(x_1\cdots x_n)=\prod_i P_{X_i}(x_i)$. On the all-zeros string the product gives $\frac{1}{2^n}$ but the true joint is $\frac12$, and for $n\ge2$ these differ. So the bits are not independent and the statement is FALSE.

Exercise 1.33.

A sequence $(X_1,\ldots,X_n)$ (with $n\ge 2$) takes only the values $00\cdots0$ and $11\cdots1$, each with probability $\frac12$. What is $P_{X_1\cdots X_n}(00\cdots0)$?

(a) $\frac12$
(b) $\frac{1}{2^n}$
(c) $\frac{1}{n}$
(d) $\frac{1}{2n}$

Show solution

The correct answer is (a) ★

The all-zeros string is one of the two atoms of the distribution, carrying probability exactly $\frac12$ by definition of the source. The value $\frac{1}{2^n}$ would hold only for a genuine i.i.d. fair source, which this is not. Hence $P_{X_1\cdots X_n}(00\cdots0)=\frac12$.

Exercise 1.34.

A sequence $(X_1,\ldots,X_n)$ takes only the values $00\cdots0$ and $11\cdots1$, each with probability $\frac12$. What is the probability that a sequence has at least one $0$ and at least one $1$?

(a) $0$
(b) $\frac12$
(c) $1-\frac{1}{2^{n-1}}$
(d) $1$

Show solution

The correct answer is (a) ★

A string has both a $0$ and a $1$ iff it is neither $00\cdots0$ nor $11\cdots1$, i.e. it lies outside the support, so each such string has probability $0$. Thus $\mathbb{P}=1-P(00\cdots0)-P(11\cdots1)=1-\frac12-\frac12=0$. The statement is TRUE.

Exercise 1.35.

A sequence $(X_1,\ldots,X_n)$ (with $n\ge 2$) takes only the values $00\cdots0$ and $11\cdots1$, each with probability $\frac12$. Is $X_1$ independent of the block $(X_2,\ldots,X_n)$?

(a) False: $X_1$ fully determines the block, so they are dependent
(b) True: $X_1$ is a single bit and the block is separate, so they are independent
(c) True: both have $\mathrm{Bernoulli}(\tfrac12)$ marginals
(d) Cannot be decided without knowing $n$

Show solution

The correct answer is (a) ★

Independence requires $P(x_1,x_2\cdots x_n)=P_{X_1}(x_1)P_{X_2\cdots X_n}(x_2\cdots x_n)$. Take $x_1=0$ and block $(1,\ldots,1)$: the joint is $0$ (no outcome mixes $0$ and $1$), but the product of marginals is $\frac12\cdot\frac12=\frac14\neq 0$. Since $X_1$ fully determines the block, they are maximally dependent, so the statement is FALSE.

Exercise 1.36.

A person is in one of three equally likely rooms $A,B,C$, encoded as $A\to 100$, $B\to 010$, $C\to 001$. Each bit passes independently through a binary asymmetric channel with $\mathbb{P}(Y=1\mid X=0)=0.1$ and $\mathbb{P}(Y=0\mid X=1)=0.2$. A reading is accepted only if the received word is one of $\{100,010,001\}$. Compute the probability of correctly reading the room.

(a) $0.648$
(b) $0.512$
(c) $0.729$
(d) $0.9$

Show solution

The correct answer is (a) ★

By symmetry, condition on room $A$ (sent word $100$). The transition table gives $\mathbb{P}(1\mid1)=0.8$, $\mathbb{P}(0\mid0)=0.9$. A correct read needs each bit unchanged: $\mathbb{P}(100\mid100)=0.8\cdot0.9\cdot0.9=0.648$. Since all rooms are symmetric, the unconditional value is the same: $\mathbf{0.648}$. ($0.512=0.8^3$ ignores the asymmetry; $0.729=0.9^3$ uses the wrong per-bit values.)

Exercise 1.37.

Same setup: rooms $A\to100$, $B\to010$, $C\to001$, each bit independent through a channel with $\mathbb{P}(Y=1\mid X=0)=0.1$, $\mathbb{P}(Y=0\mid X=1)=0.2$; a reading is accepted only if the received word is one of $\{100,010,001\}$. Compute the probability that a wrong room is accepted.

(a) $0.036$
(b) $0.018$
(c) $0.108$
(d) $0.054$

Show solution

The correct answer is (a) ★

Condition on $A$ (sent $100$). A wrong room is accepted only if the received word is $010$ or $001$. $\mathbb{P}(010\mid100)=\mathbb{P}(0\mid1)\,\mathbb{P}(1\mid0)\,\mathbb{P}(0\mid0)=0.2\cdot0.1\cdot0.9=0.018$, and likewise $\mathbb{P}(001\mid100)=0.018$. These are mutually exclusive, so add: $0.018+0.018=\mathbf{0.036}$. (Forgetting one of the two valid wrong words gives $0.018$.)

Exercise 1.38.

Same setup: rooms $A\to100$, $B\to010$, $C\to001$, each bit independent through a channel with $\mathbb{P}(Y=1\mid X=0)=0.1$, $\mathbb{P}(Y=0\mid X=1)=0.2$. Given $\mathbb{P}(\text{correct})=0.648$ and $\mathbb{P}(\text{wrong room accepted})=0.036$, compute the probability that the reading is discarded (received word not a valid codeword).

(a) $0.316$
(b) $0.352$
(c) $0.284$
(d) $0.364$

Show solution

The correct answer is (a) ★

The outcomes correct read, wrong room accepted, and discarded are mutually exclusive and exhaustive. Hence $\mathbb{P}(\text{discard})=1-0.648-0.036=\mathbf{0.316}$. Check: $0.648+2(0.018)+0.316=1.000$. (Forgetting the wrong-accept term gives $1-0.648=0.352$.)

Exercise 1.39.

A biased coin lands heads with probability $p=\tfrac{1}{10}$ and is flipped $n=200$ times independently. Let $X$ count the number of heads. Identify the distribution of $X$ and compute $\mathbb{E}[X]$ and $\operatorname{var}(X)$.

(a) $X\sim\text{Binomial}(200,\tfrac{1}{10})$, $\mathbb{E}[X]=20$, $\operatorname{var}(X)=18$
(b) $X\sim\text{Binomial}(200,\tfrac{1}{10})$, $\mathbb{E}[X]=20$, $\operatorname{var}(X)=20$
(c) $X\sim\text{Poisson}(20)$, $\mathbb{E}[X]=20$, $\operatorname{var}(X)=20$
(d) $X\sim\text{Binomial}(200,\tfrac{1}{10})$, $\mathbb{E}[X]=20$, $\operatorname{var}(X)=180$

Show solution

The correct answer is (a) ★

$X$ is a sum of $n=200$ i.i.d. Bernoulli$(p)$ indicators, hence $X\sim\text{Binomial}(n,p)$. By linearity, $\mathbb{E}[X]=np=200\cdot\tfrac{1}{10}=20$. Since the flips are independent, variances add: $\operatorname{var}(X)=np(1-p)=200\cdot\tfrac{1}{10}\cdot\tfrac{9}{10}=18$.

Exercise 1.40.

A biased coin lands heads with probability $p=\tfrac{1}{10}$, flipped $n=200$ times independently; $X$ counts heads, with $\mathbb{E}[X]=20$. Using Markov's inequality, bound $\mathbb{P}[X\geq 120]$.

(a) $\mathbb{P}[X\geq 120]\leq\tfrac{1}{6}\approx 0.167$
(b) $\mathbb{P}[X\geq 120]\leq\tfrac{120}{20}=6$
(c) $\mathbb{P}[X\geq 120]\leq\tfrac{18}{120}=0.15$
(d) $\mathbb{P}[X\geq 120]\leq\tfrac{20}{200}=0.1$

Show solution

The correct answer is (a) ★

$X\geq 0$ is non-negative, so Markov's inequality applies: $\mathbb{P}[X\geq a]\leq\mathbb{E}[X]/a$. With $a=120$ and $\mathbb{E}[X]=20$, $\mathbb{P}[X\geq 120]\leq\tfrac{20}{120}=\tfrac{1}{6}\approx 0.167$. A valid probability bound must lie in $[0,1]$, ruling out the value $6$.

Exercise 1.41.

A biased coin lands heads with probability $p=\tfrac{1}{10}$, flipped $n=200$ times independently; $X$ counts heads, with $\mathbb{E}[X]=20$ and $\operatorname{var}(X)=18$. Using Chebyshev's inequality, bound $\mathbb{P}\big[\,|X-\mathbb{E}[X]|\geq 10\,\big]$.

(a) $\mathbb{P}\big[\,|X-20|\geq 10\,\big]\leq 0.18$
(b) $\mathbb{P}\big[\,|X-20|\geq 10\,\big]\leq 1.8$
(c) $\mathbb{P}\big[\,|X-20|\geq 10\,\big]\leq 0.018$
(d) $\mathbb{P}\big[\,|X-20|\geq 10\,\big]\leq 0.5$

Show solution

The correct answer is (a) ★

Chebyshev's inequality states $\mathbb{P}\big[\,|X-\mathbb{E}[X]|\geq\varepsilon\,\big]\leq\operatorname{var}(X)/\varepsilon^{2}$. With $\operatorname{var}(X)=18$ and $\varepsilon=10$, $\mathbb{P}\big[\,|X-20|\geq 10\,\big]\leq\tfrac{18}{10^{2}}=\tfrac{18}{100}=0.18$. Note the denominator is $\varepsilon^2=100$, not $\varepsilon=10$.

Exercise 1.42.

A fair coin ($p=\tfrac{1}{2}$) is flipped $n$ times independently. Let $X_n$ be the number of heads and $S_n=X_n/n$ the sample mean. Compute $\mathbb{E}[S_n]$ and $\operatorname{var}(S_n)$.

(a) $\mathbb{E}[S_n]=\tfrac{1}{2}$, $\operatorname{var}(S_n)=\dfrac{1}{4n}$
(b) $\mathbb{E}[S_n]=\tfrac{1}{2}$, $\operatorname{var}(S_n)=\dfrac{1}{4}$
(c) $\mathbb{E}[S_n]=\tfrac{n}{2}$, $\operatorname{var}(S_n)=\dfrac{n}{4}$
(d) $\mathbb{E}[S_n]=\tfrac{1}{2}$, $\operatorname{var}(S_n)=\dfrac{1}{2n}$

Show solution

The correct answer is (a) ★

Write $X_n=\sum_{i=1}^n B_i$ with i.i.d. Bernoulli$(p)$, $p=\tfrac12$, so $\mathbb{E}[B_i]=p$ and $\operatorname{var}(B_i)=p(1-p)=\tfrac14$. By linearity $\mathbb{E}[S_n]=\tfrac1n\,np=p=\tfrac12$. By independence variances add, and $\operatorname{var}(aY)=a^2\operatorname{var}(Y)$ with $a=\tfrac1n$ gives $\operatorname{var}(S_n)=\tfrac{p(1-p)}{n}=\dfrac{1}{4n}$.

Exercise 1.43.

For the fair-coin sample mean $S_n=X_n/n$ with $\mathbb{E}[S_n]=\tfrac12$ and $\operatorname{var}(S_n)=\tfrac{1}{4n}$, use Chebyshev's inequality to give an upper bound for $\mathbb{P}\!\left[\,|S_n-\tfrac12|\geq\epsilon\,\right]$ (any $\epsilon>0$).

(a) $\mathbb{P}\!\left[\,|S_n-\tfrac12|\geq\epsilon\,\right]\leq\dfrac{1}{4n\,\epsilon^{2}}$
(b) $\mathbb{P}\!\left[\,|S_n-\tfrac12|\geq\epsilon\,\right]\leq\dfrac{1}{4n\,\epsilon}$
(c) $\mathbb{P}\!\left[\,|S_n-\tfrac12|\geq\epsilon\,\right]\leq\dfrac{1}{2n\,\epsilon^{2}}$
(d) $\mathbb{P}\!\left[\,|S_n-\tfrac12|\geq\epsilon\,\right]\leq\dfrac{1}{4\,\epsilon^{2}}$

Show solution

The correct answer is (a) ★

Chebyshev's inequality states $\mathbb{P}[\,|Y-\mathbb{E}[Y]|\geq\epsilon\,]\leq\operatorname{var}(Y)/\epsilon^2$. With $Y=S_n$ and $\operatorname{var}(S_n)=\tfrac{1}{4n}$ this gives $\mathbb{P}[\,|S_n-\tfrac12|\geq\epsilon\,]\leq\dfrac{1}{4n\,\epsilon^{2}}$. Note the bound uses $\epsilon^2$ (not $\epsilon$) in the denominator and the full variance $\tfrac{1}{4n}$.

Exercise 1.44.

For the fair-coin sample mean $S_n=X_n/n$, Chebyshev gives $\mathbb{P}\!\left[\,|S_n-\tfrac12|<\epsilon\,\right]\geq 1-\tfrac{1}{4n\epsilon^2}$ for every fixed $\epsilon>0$. Evaluate $\displaystyle\lim_{n\to\infty}\mathbb{P}\!\left[\,|S_n-\tfrac12|<\epsilon\,\right]$ and identify the law it illustrates.

(a) The limit is $1$; it illustrates the weak law of large numbers ($S_n\xrightarrow{\mathbb{P}}\tfrac12$)
(b) The limit is $0$; it illustrates the weak law of large numbers
(c) The limit is $\tfrac12$; it illustrates the central limit theorem
(d) The limit is $1$; it illustrates the central limit theorem

Show solution

The correct answer is (a) ★

For fixed $\epsilon>0$ we have the squeeze $1-\tfrac{1}{4n\epsilon^2}\leq\mathbb{P}[\,|S_n-\tfrac12|<\epsilon\,]\leq 1$. As $n\to\infty$ the lower bound $\to 1$, so the limit is $1$. Equivalently $\mathbb{P}[\,|S_n-\tfrac12|\geq\epsilon\,]\to 0$, meaning $S_n$ converges in probability to $\tfrac12$: this is the weak law of large numbers (not the CLT, which concerns the Gaussian distribution of fluctuations).

Exercise 1.45.

A binary asymmetric channel has crossover probabilities $P_{Y|X}(1\mid 0)=\delta_0$ and $P_{Y|X}(0\mid 1)=\delta_1$, with uniform input $P_X(0)=P_X(1)=\tfrac12$. The channel is used memorylessly three times with i.i.d. uniform input symbols. Compute the probability $P_{Y^3}(000)$ of receiving the all-zero output.

(a) $\bigl(\tfrac12(1-\delta_0)+\tfrac12\,\delta_1\bigr)^3$
(b) $(1-\delta_0)^3$
(c) $\tfrac12(1-\delta_0)+\tfrac12\,\delta_1$
(d) $\bigl(\tfrac12(1-\delta_0)(1-\delta_1)\bigr)^3$

Show solution

The correct answer is (a) ★

By the law of total probability and memorylessness, the three output symbols are marginally i.i.d., so $P_{Y^3}(000)=\bigl(P_Y(0)\bigr)^3$. The single-symbol marginal is $P_Y(0)=\tfrac12 P_{Y|X}(0\mid 0)+\tfrac12 P_{Y|X}(0\mid 1)=\tfrac12(1-\delta_0)+\tfrac12\,\delta_1$. Cubing gives $\bigl(\tfrac12(1-\delta_0)+\tfrac12\,\delta_1\bigr)^3$.

Exercise 1.46.

A binary asymmetric channel has $P_{Y|X}(1\mid 0)=\delta_0$ and $P_{Y|X}(0\mid 1)=\delta_1$, with uniform input $P_X(0)=P_X(1)=\tfrac12$, used memorylessly three times (i.i.d. uniform inputs). Given that $Y^3=000$ was received, compute the posterior probability $P_{X^3\mid Y^3}(000\mid 000)$ that the all-zero word was transmitted.

(a) $\Bigl(\dfrac{1-\delta_0}{(1-\delta_0)+\delta_1}\Bigr)^3$
(b) $(1-\delta_0)^3$
(c) $\dfrac{1-\delta_0}{(1-\delta_0)+\delta_1}$
(d) $\Bigl(\dfrac{1-\delta_0}{1+\delta_1}\Bigr)^3$

Show solution

The correct answer is (a) ★

By Bayes, $P_{X^3\mid Y^3}(000\mid 000)=\dfrac{P_{Y^3\mid X^3}(000\mid 000)P_{X^3}(000)}{P_{Y^3}(000)}=\dfrac{(1-\delta_0)^3\cdot\tfrac18}{\bigl(P_Y(0)\bigr)^3}$. Writing $P_Y(0)=\tfrac12[(1-\delta_0)+\delta_1]$ makes the $\tfrac18$ cancel, giving $\Bigl(\dfrac{1-\delta_0}{(1-\delta_0)+\delta_1}\Bigr)^3$, the cube of the single-symbol posterior $P_{X\mid Y}(0\mid 0)$.
